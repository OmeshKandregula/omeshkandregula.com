Summary

An RSVP-based reading tool grounded in peer-reviewed research, built because I kept failing to get through books and articles I actually needed to read.

Context

Founder · Product + Engineering Early Prototype · Ongoing

Started after finding a 2023 PubMed study showing RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation) improves reading comprehension for ADHD users by 13%. Built the first version in a weekend using Claude Code and Lovable.

The Problem

I need to read. Books, research papers, long Substack pieces. I struggle to get through them:

  • Attention drifts mid-paragraph, even on content I care about.
  • Traditional reading apps offer no mechanism to fight this.
  • Willpower-based approaches keep failing at the same places.
  • Most "focus" tools treat the symptom instead of the reading mechanics.

What I Built

Two core features, shipped in early prototype form.

  • RSVP reader: words flash one at a time, no eye movement needed
  • Body-doubling rooms: read with a friend or accountability partner
  • Paste or URL import: low-friction content entry

Product Thinking

Research first, convention second. Most reading apps optimize for library management, not the act of reading.

RSVP removes the "where to look" decision. Body-doubling adds social accountability without requiring coordination.

No AI summaries. That would defeat the point.

The Research

Why RSVP instead of just building a timer or a focus mode.

The bottleneck in reading isn't vocabulary or intelligence. It's attention fragmentation during the physical act of scanning text.

Saccades are the rapid eye movements your eyes make while reading. Every saccade is an opportunity for attention to drift before you land on the next word. RSVP eliminates this by flashing words one at a time in a fixed position. Your eyes don't move. The cognitive task reduces to comprehension alone.

Key Finding

"Reading without eye movements: Improving reading comprehension in ADHD" (PubMed, 2023) showed a 13% improvement in comprehension scores for ADHD users when reading via RSVP versus standard text. The effect was most pronounced in longer passages.

What the research suggested for product design

Speed is personal: let the user control WPM
The reading window should have zero UI around it
Comprehension over speed: start slow, let users go faster
Pause and rewind are non-negotiable features

Feature Architecture

RSVP Engine

  • Words flash one at a time in a fixed center position. No eye tracking. No scrolling.
  • User controls words-per-minute from 150 to 600. Speed has a direct effect on comprehension, so this matters.
  • Pause, rewind by 10 words, and restart are the only UI elements during a session.

Body-Doubling Rooms

  • Share a room link with a friend or accountability partner. Both start reading (same content or different).
  • Presence creates focus. No chat, no interaction during the session. Just the awareness of another person reading.
  • Inspired by the ADHD body-doubling technique, adapted for asynchronous-friendly use.

Content Import

  • Paste plain text or a URL. The tool strips formatting and sends clean text to the RSVP renderer.
  • Works with Substack, blog posts, book excerpts, research papers.
  • No library, no sync, no account needed to try it. Friction was the enemy of the first session.

Design Decisions

What I deliberately left out matters as much as what I built.

Kept

Minimal reading UI

During a session, the only things visible are the current word and basic controls. Everything else is hidden. The RSVP method works precisely because there is nothing else competing for attention.

Kept

Presence-only rooms

No chat during sessions. No commenting. Just a "friend is reading" indicator. Adding communication would have turned this into a social product, which would have broken the focus mechanic.

Cut

AI summaries

The obvious feature to add would have defeated the purpose. The goal is to help you read and retain, not to read for you. I will not build this.

Deferred

Progress tracking

Reading streaks and metrics could add engagement. Deferred because I want to validate that the core reading experience works first. Gamification on a broken core is just noise.

What's Next

Questions I'm still trying to answer.

The current version is a working prototype. I'm using it myself and sharing it with a few people who have the same problem. Before building more, I want to answer:

01

Does body-doubling compound the RSVP effect?

The paper validates RSVP alone. No research I found tested RSVP plus social presence together. That's the hypothesis I want to test.

02

Who actually has this problem badly enough to change their behavior?

I need to talk to more people. Specifically people who are trying to read professional content and failing to finish it, not casual readers.

03

Is a browser extension the right form factor?

Right now it's a web app you go to. Reading happens everywhere. The better path might be meeting users where the content lives rather than making them come to Fovea.

"Sometimes the best product insight is a paper you found at 2am trying to understand why you couldn't read."

This is still early. But the research is real, the problem is real, and the first version works for me. That's enough to keep going.
