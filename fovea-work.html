Fovea

Speed reading built for ADHD brains, not just fast ones

Personal Project Active Development · PWA · Built with Lovable + Claude Code

Named after the fovea centralis, the part of your retina responsible for sharp central vision. That is exactly where the reader keeps your gaze.

Summary

An RSVP speed reading web app built specifically for people with ADHD, grounded in peer-reviewed research and designed around how ADHD brains actually fail at reading.

Context

Founder · Product + Engineering Active · Phase 1 Live

A personal problem I kept failing to solve with willpower. Started after finding a 2023 PubMed study showing RSVP improves ADHD reading comprehension by nearly 13%. Runs in the browser and installs as a PWA on iPad, iPhone, or desktop. No App Store required.

The Problem

Most people with ADHD don't have a reading speed problem. They have a staying-on-the-page problem.

  • Eyes drift and the mind wanders mid-sentence, even on content they care about.
  • Re-reading the same paragraph four times, then closing the book feeling worse.
  • Existing speed reading apps are built for people who already read fine and want to go faster.
  • No app was built for people who struggle to stay on the page at all.

The Research

Why RSVP specifically, and what the ADHD finding means for product design.

The bottleneck in ADHD reading isn't vocabulary or intelligence. It's attention fragmentation at the physical level of scanning text.

Saccades are the rapid eye movements your eyes make while reading across a line. Every saccade is an opportunity for attention to drift before landing on the next word. RSVP eliminates this entirely by flashing words one at a time in a fixed center position. Your eyes don't move. The cognitive task reduces to comprehension alone.

Key Finding

A 2023 PubMed study tested RSVP specifically on ADHD adults and found they comprehended nearly 13% better with RSVP than with normal text. The critical detail: neurotypical readers showed no improvement, or a slight decline at speed. The benefit is specific to the population with the attention architecture problem.

The ORP Insight

Research on reading mechanics shows the brain spends roughly 80% of reading time just locating the Optimal Recognition Point (the specific letter in each word that triggers recognition) and only 20% processing meaning. RSVP with ORP alignment collapses that 80% to zero, freeing the full cognitive load for comprehension.

What the research shaped in the product

ORP letter highlighted in coral red at a fixed screen position — eyes never move
Speed is personal: new users start at 80% of their baseline WPM, not a fixed default
The reading window has zero competing UI during a session
Pause and rewind are non-negotiable — zoning out is assumed, not punished

The Reading Engine

Every detail in the RSVP reader is a deliberate design decision, not a default.

ORP + Smart Pacing

  • Each word is aligned so the ORP letter sits at a fixed screen position, highlighted in coral red. Your eyes genuinely never move.
  • Words don't all get the same display time. Longer words get more milliseconds. Commas and semicolons add a brief pause. Sentence-ending punctuation gets a longer pause. Paragraph breaks get the longest pause with a visual divider.
  • Speed range: 100 to 1,200 WPM via a slider. App ramps from 80% of baseline over the first 30 seconds to ease in.

Context + Modes

  • Peripheral context: the previous word fades at 30% opacity to the left, the next at 15% to the right. Reduces the anxiety of feeling completely lost in a sentence.
  • Chunk mode: toggle to display 2 or 3 words at a time for users who comprehend better with phrases.
  • Bionic Reading toggle: the first half of every word is bolded, helping the eye anchor faster on each word as it flashes.

Text-to-Speech

  • Built on the Web Speech API, no external service or cost. Two modes: Word mode speaks each word as it flashes; Sentence mode reads full sentences aloud while the RSVP continues, with the current word highlighted in real time.
  • Voice, pitch, rate, and volume are all configurable. On iPad, all system voices are available.
  • Ambient sound fades automatically to 20% when voiceover is active.

ADHD-Specific Features

The features that separate Fovea from every other RSVP app. Each one is justified by an ADHD failure mode, not a generic UX pattern.

Recover Button

A large, always-visible button labeled "Recover." Tap it (or press R) and the reader quietly rewinds 10 seconds. No judgment, no friction, no animation. ADHD users will zone out. The product question is whether recovering is frictionless enough that they don't just close the app.

Hyperfocus Mode

Full screen, no UI, nothing competing for attention. Optionally prompts the user to enable Do Not Disturb before starting. When the only thing on screen is the word, there is nothing to context-switch to.

Body Doubling Rooms (Phase 2)

Optional virtual co-reading sessions. Users see a live count of other people reading at the same time. No chat, no video, just the awareness that others are present. Body doubling is a well-documented ADHD productivity technique — the presence of others, even silent and remote, measurably improves focus.

Pomodoro Reading

Built-in 25-minute sessions with 5-minute breaks. After each break, an interstitial shows what was just accomplished ("You read 6,200 words") and counts down to the next sprint. Bounded sessions are easier to commit to than open-ended ones.

Ambient Soundscapes (Phase 2)

Brown noise, rain, cafe, lofi, fireplace, white noise. Volume-adjustable. Fades down automatically when voiceover is on. Research consistently shows ADHD brains focus better with consistent background sound than silence or variable noise.

Streak Freezes

A daily reading streak that auto-protects on missed days using earned "freeze" tokens (one per week of consistent reading). The freeze prevents the "I already broke it, forget it" spiral that derails ADHD users from any habit-building attempt. Missing a day doesn't end the streak — it costs a token.

Smart Session Length

The app tracks how long users typically read before they bail. It suggests sessions of that length plus 10%, with specific framing: "Today, let's aim for 14 minutes" rather than an open-ended session timer. Specificity beats aspiration for ADHD.

XP, Quests, and Leveling

Three daily rotating quests ("Read for 10 minutes before noon"). Session XP from words read, quiz scores, and session length. Level progression unlocks themes and sounds. Kept subtle — a progress bar in the corner, not a wall of notifications. The point is to make showing up feel like progress, not performance.

Content Support

All formats feed into the same RSVP reader with the same controls. The ingestion layer is where most of the real engineering work lives.

PDF

Parsed client-side with pdfjs-dist. Rule-based cleaning strips page numbers, repeated headers and footers, table of contents pages, bibliography sections, and publisher boilerplate before the RSVP reader ever touches it. An AI pass handles anything the rules miss. Word count before and after is shown: "Removed 4,200 words of boilerplate."

EPUB

Parsed with epubjs. Extracts chapters in order with titles and cover art. Front and back matter (copyright page, about the author, index) is stripped automatically. Chapter order follows the native spine.

URL Import

Paste any Substack, Medium, or article URL. Mozilla Readability extracts the main content and strips navigation, ads, and subscribe CTAs. Substack-specific patterns (share prompts, subscriber banners) are handled with separate rules.

Chapter Navigation

For EPUBs, chapters come from the native spine. For PDFs, heuristics detect chapter boundaries using all-caps lines, "Chapter/Part/Section" patterns, font size metadata, and lines alone at page breaks. If fewer than 3 chapters are detected, the PDF splits into ~3,000-word segments and the user is told which method was used.

Chapter Sidebar

Collapsible panel showing every chapter with title, estimated read time at the user's current WPM, and status (not started, in progress, completed, skipped). On iPad landscape it's a left sidebar; on portrait and mobile, a bottom drawer. Opening it doesn't pause reading.

AI Features (Phase 2)

Auto-summary checkpoints every 10 minutes or at chapter ends. Comprehension quizzes (3 questions, skippable). Highlight-and-ask: tap any word to get a definition or plain-language explanation of the surrounding paragraph. All AI results cached per document so the model runs once, not on every read.

Design

Every visual decision was made for ADHD users specifically: reduce stimulation during reading, add warmth during everything else.

Color

Near-black + warm golden yellow

Background is #0C0C10 — near-black with a barely perceptible blue undertone. Primary accent is warm golden yellow, a reading lamp color rather than the default productivity blue. The ORP focal letter is coral red with a subtle radial glow.

Typography

Fraunces + geometric sans + JetBrains Mono

Fraunces as the display font — a variable optical-size serif with genuine character. Geometric sans for UI elements. JetBrains Mono for numbers and stats. Warm white text, not pure white.

Texture + Motion

Noise layer + Framer Motion philosophy

A 3 to 5% opacity noise texture sits over every surface. It's subtle enough that you don't consciously notice it, but it makes the app feel designed rather than generated. Animations: transitions at 150-200ms, meaningful moments at 400-600ms, nothing above 700ms. The RSVP word fades in over 20ms and cuts out instantly — any fade-out would interrupt reading rhythm.

Accessibility

Low-stim mode

A settings toggle that disables confetti, animated transitions, and ambient sound prompts for users with sensory sensitivities. ADHD and sensory processing sensitivities often co-occur. This was not an afterthought.

"Most people with ADHD don't have a reading speed problem. They have a staying-on-the-page problem."

That reframe changed what I built. Every app I looked at was optimized for people who already read fine. Fovea is optimized for people who can't stay on the page. The research validated the approach. The product question then became: what else does that population need that a speed reading app doesn't think to solve? That's where the Recover button came from, where the Streak Freezes came from, where Low-stim mode came from. The product exists because I stopped asking what features to add and started asking which failure modes to design around.
